Break a leg!

2024 seems to be a defining year for many, and if March is any indication for the rest of my year, then I'm going to have a hell of year. If you have not yet received an unsolicited foot pic from me over the past week, then here's the news: I broke my foot.

It sucks, and I'd prefer a world in which my foot wasn't broken. But also, I find solace in the idea that this was bound to happen (although my parents would vehemently disagree). Perhaps that's excusatory and too deterministic, but the same effort and approach I had taken the past 10 months of climbing had left me largely unscathed, and nothing differed the day I broke my foot... aside from the fact that I went rock climbing during my lunch break, but I will never accept this experience as a lesson regarding the consequences of shirking from work.

Still, it's interesting to reconcile an outcome I'd now consider "inevitable" with the the short-term outlook I had on these upcoming months prior to the accident. I had plans to travel, get back into running, climb more intentionally, etc, and each of these will take a momentary pause for the next couple of months. So, how can I reconcile an inevitability with an outlook that was entirely contradictory to it? Is that delusion? The miscalculation of risk? I don't know. Things don't need to make sense, I suppose.

Regardless, I now have to wait. For what? The future version of myself that I've envisioned. Whether that be the version of myself going back to Austin and feeling the warmth of 80 degree weather with a margarita in hand, ranchera music blasting, and the mist of water spraying from a fan overhead. Or, visiting Knoxville for the first time to see my friend who is recently married, meeting his wife, having a mild allergy attack from his cats, and sitting in their apartment with them on a late evening, watching their life happen in real-time and fathom how much our lives differ. Or, spending the week in a remote town of New Hampshire to go climbing after a day of work, appreciating how much slower life feels away from the city, and questioning if I'd live away from a large city for a bit.

I'm sure two things are apparent now. One, I had a handful of trips I was jazzed about, and two, I tend to romanticize the future a lot... So, who is to say I can't romanticize the future of myself that involves crutches and a boot. Well, sure I can. So here's my attempt, albeit a somewhat reluctant one.

There will be evenings where I'm reading a good book on my couch, warm lighting illuminating the pages as dusks sets and natural light dissipates from the room. Perhaps I'll watch a good movie after or keep watching the new tv series I've recently gotten into. Or, maybe delivery is on its way from my favorite sushi place. Or, there will be evenings where people come over and we're enjoying eating, cooking, drinking, talking, smoking, board gaming, video gaming, movie watching, music listening, or most other pleasant things that end with -ing. Or even better, there will be days where friends can help me take out my trash... please guys I have no way of taking out this trash right now, and it's starting to scare me.

Catalog of Unabashed Gratitude by Ross Gay

And thank you the baggie of dreadlocks I found in a drawer
while washing and folding the clothes of our murdered friend;
the photo in which his arm slung
around the sign to “the trail of silences”; thank you
the way before he died he held
his hands open to us; for coming back
in a waft of incense or in the shape of a boy
in another city looking
from between his mother’s legs,
or disappearing into the stacks after brushing by;
for moseying back in dreams where,
seeing us lost and scared
he put his hand on our shoulders
and pointed us to the temple across town;

So, I didn't read a book this week, and I didn't read this poem this week either. But, it is a poem I have read, and I think it's quite topical given the events of this past week.

Aside from its subject matter, I really like this poem because of the breadth of media it's available in. There's a YouTube video, audio recordings, and a version on Spotify with a backtrack from Bon Iver. My favorite is the YouTube video. It's 14 minutes long. It's worth it, and it's very accessible for those who don't like to read. If I could, I think I'd listen to it on a long subway ride, the ethereal backtrack and soothing vocal contrasting with the stress, noises, and rats of NYC's subway system.

And if you don't plan on listening, here's the last stanza:

5 Hours at Mt Sinai

So I lost two of my toes.

Admittedly, I've been a bit melodramatic about the foot injury. After all, foot injuries are common; it'll only be a few months; I have a comfortable space with food & electronics; and, I have health insurance. This is less like the end of the world, and more akin to being a 10yr old kid stuck at home most of the time feeling incredibly bored (...and the boredom has already begun).

Also, emergency rooms do an incredible job of giving perspective to life's woes. When I walked into the emergency room, my own emergency felt like the end of the world. Perhaps selfish, but perhaps for the sake of my own self-preservation, my issue felt like a really big deal. But then I look around and realized that my big deal is comparatively, a quite little deal. I know it's important to not diminish your own struggles, but in that moment I was incredibly grateful that I was only there for a broken foot. Around me were the myriad of emergencies people were undergoing, and here I was with a funny story about rock climbing during my lunch break.

I remember the front desk woman asking what my purpose of visit was. She seemed drained of emotion, eyes darting across the screen. "I think I broke my foot", I told her. Her expression softened, she looked at my foot, and said "awww, I'm sorry". The change of expression stuck with me. I think it's because my situation was relatable, minor, and on the easier side of cases that likely come through. My broken foot was just a minor inconvenience, and it absolutely is. Her response was hilarious. It was also reassuring. I try to make jokes and laugh about shitty situations, so it was oddly comforting that the hospital staff thought it was funny too. Mind you, the resident making my splint said, "That's such a tech bro thing, to break your foot while rock climbing during work", unprompted. I enjoyed that I was providing comedic relief to myself, the environment, and the staff around me. My toxic trait was that I wanted to be the affable, 'easy-to-solve' patient. But maybe that's not necessarily a "toxic" thing to do. I think I just wanted to feel like my problem was not a problem at all.

Ultimately, hospitals are testaments to the unfortunate volatility of the human experience. In 5 hours, I had seen and heard quite a handful of things which made me realize how inconsequential my own problem was.

I remember the man in front of me waiting to check-in. He could barely stand and was having trouble breathing. When the staff saw him and offered him a wheelchair, all he could said was "please". I learned he was peeing and coughing up blood, as he detailed these things to the lady at the front desk. By the time I wheelchaired past him in in the waiting room, his eyes were closed and he had an IV running through him.

I remember the woman having an anxiety attack in her waiting room chair with her dog in her lap. She couldn't communicate to the hospital staff and they had no idea whether she was on drugs or not. Eventually, she calmed down, got up, and told them she's fine, thanking them for their help. She walked out.

I remember the doctor telling another patient that her husband's foot could not be operated on since the surgery took place at a Brooklyn hospital, and she'd need to take him there. She was stressed out, pissed off, and told the doctor "I'm on the verge of having a panic attack and we live here in Manhattan. How do you expect me to drag my husband all the way to East Brooklyn on the train just because the accident happened over there? Don't you all study the same stuff?". Honestly, she was right. She proceeded to shit on the doctor aloud, saying things like "So you're gonna charge him, but you can't help him"? I don't blame her.

I remember another woman sitting behind me while waiting to see a doctor the 3rd time. She was describing to the doctor her symptoms and how she couldn't make sense of it. Three times in the past couple of weeks, she'd feel hot, her legs would shake, and she'd collapse on the floor. She didn't know what was happening. The doctor asked of her family history, and she proceeded to detail her family members and their causes of her death. Abnormal heart conditions, old age, car accident. I remember her taking a phone call, calling a family member, and crying. She was scared to go outside because she didn't want to suddenly collapse on the street again.

I remember sitting in my wheelchair, awaiting my radiology results. It was 7pm; I had no service; and I was alone with my thoughts. The male nurse crossed the room and approached the pair of socks visible to me from behind the curtains of the adjoining room. The socks were a bright, lively orange. I heard the nurse scan the patient's wristband and say, "oh, it's your birthday. Happy Birthday." I heard the voice of an older man respond, "huh?". There was a pause, and the nurse responded with, "let's get you going", wheeling away the orange socks.

Things will be fine.

Thank you.

By  Julian Ordaz
