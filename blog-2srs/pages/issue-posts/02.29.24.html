When I was younger, I really wanted to leave El Paso.

I don't know what happened or what clicked, but at some point in my early childhood I told myself "I want to get out of here". It's not as if I had some awful childhood, I think I just had a really boring one. Some people look back on their childhoods fondly and have a sense of nostalgia for them, recalling family recipes, annual vacations, or holiday traditions. But I don't. Is that unfortunate? Well, I've normalized it... so I'd like to hope what's normal to me is not unfortunate for others.

"Memory is won or lost on one criterion: Do you leave the formation of memories to happenstance, or do you decide to remember?"
-Gabrielle Zevin

To which I say, what is there to remember of my childhood? Hamburger helper meals? Spending most days with my grandma after school who I couldn't speak Spanish with? Waiting years to convince my parents to let me play video games? Hearing "don't worry about it" when I often asked my brother what he was up to? My parents sending me off to hangout with the neighbor's kid who I really didn't fuck with? Being dragged to confirmation? Or Boy Scouts? All in all, I remember most days after school thinking, "I'm soooooooooooo bored." There was nothing particularly memorable, and therefore, not much that I can remember. I don't want to be melodramatic, but I do feel like life only started getting interesting once I started high school. From my own experience, high school came with much more autonomy and "firsts": the ability to drive, picking your electives, first relationships, leaving campus for lunch, high school bonfires in the desert, the prospect of applying to colleges and moving away, and band kid culture. Public education is just one big social experiment, and high school was the epitome of it.

But I had a fun time. Prior to then, I was but a sentient little boy who was confined to school and home for most of his life. School was filled with schoolwork, and home was filled with gaming - that was about it. So at what point did it click that El Paso wasn't for me? Logically, it seems that high school was the eye opener, but I do think this transformation was much earlier. In fact, I remember when I was still in Elementary school, and my family and I went to some sort of event being hosted at UTEP. The President of the university was there, she came around to our table and asked me, "Will you be coming to UTEP?". To which I said, "No. Harvard." Thankfully, I didn't qualify If I was referring to undergrad or not. But point being, even as a child I was ready to leave El Paso. Perhaps high school solidified this decision; If life became so much more interesting with this much more autonomy, how much life was living itself away from me? It certainly couldn't be here in El Paso.

So I went to UT. And wore "I'm from El Paso" like a badge of honor, and still do. I still stand by my hometown pride, it's not ironic. I suppose it's the type of relationship where only I'm allowed to hate on it. Why did I change my perspective on El Paso? Well, although I came from a fairly uneventful city like El Paso, it could've been much worse. After all, some people have to say they're from Dallas or Houston, and I wouldn't ever wish that on anyone.

Now, I'm in NYC. So, what's next?

Tomorrow, and Tomorrow, and Tomorrow by Gabrielle Zevin

The most intimate relationships allow for a great deal of privacy within them.

I judged a book by its cover, and I've learned my lesson. I'd known about this book for a couple months before finally reading it, and I held off because I thought it'd be gimmicky (especially with The Great Wave off Kanagawa as its title cover). But I was wrong. I'm a sucker for a romance and/or tragedy, and this book certainly covered its bases. One chapter into the book, I knew it was going to fuck me up. The best kind of book.

When writing these "reviews", I've learned I don't care much to review them. Working as a critic across any creative medium seems really unappealing. But, I suppose I'm not quite sure if a critic is valued for their objective sense of an art's worth, or if they're valued for their distinct perspective and input. I.e. If you subscribe to a critic's review, is the art's worth now validated in your eyes, or do you value the critic's review independently of any worth you might assign to the art? Point being, I've tried to be semi-descriptive about the books I've read in past blog posts, sometimes describing setting, characters, or plot... but I'm going to stop doing that. That information is repetitious and can be googled, my thoughts and feelings can't... unless you specifically google this blog site.

But... I will do it this one last time.

Tomorrow, Tomorrow, Tomorrow is a bildungsroman of three game developers, interlaced with their perspectives on life as told through gaming tropes.

Anyway, my favorite part of the book is when Sam asks Sadie,

Why do you think we never got together?

To which Sadie says,

Sammy, we were together. How can you not know this? Lovers are... common. I loved working with you better than I liked the idea of making love to you.

I've always placed romantic relationships on a pedestal. To me, there couldn't possibly be a more profound and intimate relationship that two people could share with one another. After all (and if all goes well), there'll only be one person you share the rest of your life with. Friendships can be incredibly meaningful as well, but there's a level of familiarity and openness that comes with romantic relationships. Tomorrow, Tomorrow, Tomorrow challenged my perspective. I'm much more conflicted about how I value romantic relationships. Maybe it's not the most meaningful relationship someone can share with another. I've often thought about romantic partners as the person who completes you, but perhaps it's ok if that can be found elsewhere and in someone else. What I enjoyed about the book is that it wasn't suggesting that friendship is what trumps romance either, rather that there are relationships that are beyond friendship, romance, and family. I think the word I'm looking for is soulmate, and for some reason, I've always felt my soulmate needed to be my romantic partner as well. But if they aren't, then what does that leave my romantic partner to be in my life?

Exodus by Bob Marley and The Wailers

I saw the Bob Marley movie over a week ago. It was fun.

It wasn't as a good as I was hoping it to be (although the 42% Rotten Tomatoes score should've prepared me), but it was fun to get excited about the movie and rally a group of people to come watch it. I enjoyed making an event of it more than the movie itself. Honestly, if the movie didn't keep making a really weird metaphor to Teddy Roosevelt on a horse in the middle of a field alit with flames, then it'd be a much better movie. Full disclosure, it was not actually Teddy Roosevelt, but it did feel like a less comical version of Robin William's Teddy Roosevelt in Night at the Museum. Quick aside, but Night at the Museum had a stacked cast: Ben Stiller, Robin Williams, Owen Wilson, Rami Malek, Ricky Gervais, Bill Hader, Paul Rudd, Hugh Jackman, and Jonah Hill (allegedly). I haven't seen it in a while, but it occupies the same place in my mind as "Elf" with Will Ferrell - early 2000s comfort movies.

At some point I stopped playing games.

I used to be a gamer.

When I was younger, I often spent my free time playing video games. Little Big Planet, Skyrim, Fallout, Garry's Mod, Terraria, Minecraft - not an exhaustive list, but these games often composed the memories of my childhood. I played shooters like Call of Duty and Battlefield as well, but they were not healthy for me to play given my competitive nature. I remember my family telling me stories of how I'd stand up during a match and stare closely into the screen, intensely absorbed in wanting to win. Can you blame me? Shooters were a drug, and I'm sure game developers created them with that purpose in mind. 10 fast-paced minutes of rapid eye movement, finger twitching, and the satisfaction of coming at the top of the leaderboard. Sweet victory, to be rewarded with a new camo skin.

I'm smart, and I'm competitive. I wonder if these traits predate my interest in games, or if games enabled and encouraged them.

Regarding my competitiveness, most definitely the latter. I'm the youngest in my family (my brother and most cousins being 7+ years older), and I distinctly remember my brother & cousin consistently wrecking me in any competitive video game we played. There was no mercy, and I probably cried a handful of times. If I wanted to beat them, I had to truly beat them. You can imagine my satisfaction when I would wreck my brother in FIFA several years later and run up the score... talk about playing the long game. So, if you ever play a boardgame, card game, or video game with me, you'll probably notice that I like to take it easy and be easygoing about it, otherwise I'm going to run up my cortisol levels trying to win. It's better this way, otherwise you might find yourself losing to me in a game you've spent your entire childhood playing (sorry Nikhil). Perhaps that's why I identify as outwardly Type B and inwardly Type A.

Regarding the former, I'm not going to stroke my ego, but I think it's interesting that I'm as studious of a person as I am. I never felt pressured as a child by my parents to do well in school. In fact, I remember the first time I told my parents I was drinking in college while still underage, and they were worried that I had lost all drive in school and would go off the rails. It was a well-intentioned concern, but I remember feeling upset, and it was because I didn't like my ambition being doubted. I got defensive, and I remember saying something along the lines of, "You don't have to tell me to be ambitious and want a better life. I've always wanted it for myself." I suppose my competitive nature drove my studiousness, but I do like to learn things for the sake of knowing them as well. That's why I was interested in Philosophy, but then realized that a lot of people who make a profession of Philosophy are really obnoxious and self-righteous. It's the PURSUIT of wisdom! You're never going to find the answer, that's kind of the whole point. Anyway, how does gaming tie into this?

Well, I've realized that coding is the most interesting outlet for me when it comes to learning. It's the first time since playing open-world games like Minecraft & Terraria, where I could learn something (e.g. how to craft wood planks) and then create something 'real' (e.g. a two-story wooden house with a fireplace). After all, my username in Minecraft was The_Builder14, so there's likely always been this inclination for me to build 'things'. You can't do that in finance, accounting, consulting, [insert traditional business career path]. Maybe you make financial models in these career paths, but the impact isn't real. You're creating 'perceived impact'. The quality of my work is likely evaluated by how much someone likes its assumptions. My gripe is that it's not quantifiable. Obviously you're making 'something' in most career paths, but what gives me satisfaction is if this 'something' can be objectively evaluated. The impact needs to be quantifiable. If you can't measure it, you can't manage it. I don't subscribe to this rigid framework in other facets of life, but if I'm committed to working 40 hours a week for the next 20 years of my life in exchange for a salary, then I need my impact to be measurable and compensated accordingly.
