Day 10 - Three.js Sprint

Today I learned...

  • Three.js boilerplate code involves establishing a scene, camera, renderer, and animation loop. A scene is where you .add() all objects, light, and cameras; a camera establishes the FOV, aspect ratio, and positioning within a scene and its objects; the renderer uses WebGL to render 3D graphics and can be appended to the relevant element in the DOM to establish sizing; and, the animation loop is a function which refreshes the screen, assigns animations to objects, and renders via the .render(scene, camera) method.
  • Within a scene, objects and 3D models both refer to elements that can be added to a scene; more specifically, objects refer to basic geometric shapes (e.g. cubes, spheres, and planes) that exist within the 3.js library and can be simply created by defining an object's geometry, its material (i.e texture), combining the two via: 'const shape = THREE.Mesh(geometry, material)', and adding to the scene via 'scene.add(shape).'
  • As for 3D models: (1) these are more intricate 3D objects typically rendered in an animation software like Blender; (2) they are very expensive to purchase for commerical use, but some can be found for free at Sketchfab; (3) Three.js recommends the glTF (GL Transmission Format) for 3D models, which can subsequently be loaded into a scene via the GLTFLoader(); (4) some adjustments may need to be made to the object's positioning, camera angle, and lighting to ensure that the 3D object is vsisible within the webpage.
