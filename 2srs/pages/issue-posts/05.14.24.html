Sort of. My doctor cleared me to walk. But walking is still very hard.

So, I'm still in my boot for the next few days as I continue to try putting weight on my foot. Regardless, having to use only one leg for the past two months was actually one of the worst experiences I've gone through in recent memory. If I had to choose between experiencing the pain I felt when first breaking my foot or the following 8 monotonous weeks, I'd happily break my foot again & again & again & again.

Prior to breaking my foot, I never wished for a broken bone, but I always had a curiosity about what it'd be like (and what bone I'd break). Retrospectively, this curiosity was misguided; regardless of what the quote above may suggest, there is no positive way to spin breaking your foot.

However, I will admit that there are probably way better bones to break and still live your life as normal - a broken foot is not one of them. Additionally, I'm a bit afraid of breaking bones going forward. Not because of the pain or the surgeries, but because of the amount of time in my life I'd lose out on; breaking my foot felt like a 2 month pause from my life, and it's still really frustrating to think about the time lost.

Anyway, I'm still not very mobile, and honestly, I'm still much quicker on crutches right now. Regardless, progress is being made - what a fucking relief.

Wendy's 4 for 4

I didn't have a chance to read a full book as of late, so I've opted for a collection of short stories I read in the meanwhile. I'm going to spoil all of them - otherwise you could probably read through all of these in like an hour and a half. Here we go.

The Ones Who Walk Away from Omelas by Ursula Le Guin

Happiness is based on a just discrimination of what is necessary, what is neither necessary nor destructive, and what is destructive.

This story was fine. It's pretty revered in literature for some reason, but maybe that's because it takes inspiration from Dostoevsky? The whole story is about a city's happiness powered by the suffering of one child locked away underground. Is it justified? Is it inevitable? What is life like outside the city? Why leave the city? Is the comfort of some only guaranteed by the suffering of others? Isn't happiness all that matters? I'm assuming these are the questions your English teacher would ask you.

My cacao teacher from Guatemala would argue "yes, happiness is all that matters." And utilitarians would add, "yes, the suffering is justified in favor of the majority." Boom, problem solved.

There Will Come Soft Rains by Ray Bradbury

Time had become surreal in the absence of human presence, slowing down and stretching into eternity.

After Fashrenheit 451, this is the 2nd thing I've read by Ray Bradbury - I like his work. For some reason, Ray Bradbury occupies the same space in my head as George Orwell, Kurt Vonnegut, Joseph Heller. Maybe they all took inspiration from each others?

Anyway, for a story seemingly devoid of any real plot, I'm surprised I enjoyed this one as much as I did. The whole story just details a smart house in 2026, years after a nuclear explosion. No one's survived, and there's no other houses still standing. It's just this house, and it continues to perform its automated tasks long after anyone is around. It's very ominous and places the reader in a time and space where they no longer matter. I think most post-apocalpytic stories still feature humans in some capacity, so it was really interesting to read a story devoid of people.

The Last Question by Isaac Asimov

Individual science fiction stories may seem as trivial as ever to the blinder critics and philosophers of today, but the core of science fiction - its essence - has become crucial to our salvation, if we are to be saved at all.

This story is about 10 pages of really boring build just to climax in the very last sentence. Stories are an investment of time, I get it, but this easily could've been shortened to 3 paragraphs.

I'm not going to try understand the science/theory as I explain it, but pretty much humans build a superintelligent computer that advances until the end of time. Everyone keeps asking it how to prevent the end of the universe, and it is only until the very literal end of time (devoid of humans), that it has the answer. It's answer is, "LET THERE BE LIGHT".

I like the idea though. We created God, and God created us? We created ourselves? The universe is cylical? Fun thoughts.

A Good Man is Hard to Find by Flannery O'Connor

"If you would pray," the old lady said, "Jesus would help you."
"That's right," The Misfit Said.
"Well then, why don't you pray?" she asked trembling with delight suddenly.
"I don't want no help", he said. "I'm doing all right by myself."

This story is continued proof that the Southern Gothic genre is cool af. Tldr: protagonist gets murdered by a serial killer. Really interesting from a storytelling perspective since you're not often expecting the protagonist to "lose".

Also, just read the quote above again. That's such a sick piece of dialogue. The old lady is standing before a serial killer, and she trembles with delight at the hope that she's convinced The Misfit to be a holy man and spare her life.

OSLO, AUGUST 31st by Joachim Trier

Anders : It will get better. Everything will be alright.
[laughing]
Anders : Except it won't, you know.

Ok, I would not recommend watching this movie, only because it is fucking tragic to watch unfold, but maybe that's on me for watching this movie late at night.

Oslo, August 31st is the 2nd movie in Trier's Oslo Trilogy. The 1st being Reprised and the 3rd being The Worst Person in the World. Interestingly, all feature Anders Danielsen Lie as a lead role across the series' 18 years, uniquely capturing him and his characters at different points in their life. He doesn't play the same character across the series, but his age is often a talking point in each of the movies I've seen so far. Lastly, he's an incredible actor with no formal training, and his primary profession is as a doctor in Oslo in-between movies. Incredible.

I'm not trying to compare myself to an Academy-award nominated director, but if I was to make a movie, it'd be in the style of Joachim Trier - across the two movies I've seen, there's a a very raw portrayal of the human experience, depicting flawed protagonists undergoing existential problems in their urban lifestyles. Throw in a couple of literary quotes into the dialogue as well and that's essentially my style of movie.

For context & spoilers, Oslo, August 31st captures the full day of a recovering drug addict, Anders, leaving rehab to visit his family and friends in Oslo. Throughout this day, you slowly watch Anders life fall apart as he struggles to come to terms with the life that awaits him outside of rehab, having spent the past 6 years using and dealing Heroin. My favorite scene from the movie is 'the cafe scene', where Anders sits in a cafe and hears the droning of conversations and life around him. I love this scene because it's the best depiction of loneliness that I've ever seen in media.

I'd highly recommend watching the cafe scene on YouTube. I would not highly recommend watching the whole movie.

Another Short Story

Final note on my feet, but I think the photo above is so fascinating because not only am I standing up again, but you can literally see the muscular degeneration in my left thigh based on the wrinkling in my pant leg... crazy how you need to use your muscles to keep them.

Anyway, I've recognized lately that I'm not really getting my "writing fix" from blogs. Not that I don't enjoy typing up blogs, but they're much more journalistic & casual than any genuine attempts at creative writing. To some extent, these blogs are stylistically similar to if I sent someone a really long text message chain (i.e. I'm not really challening myself to write well). That's not to say that my blogs are shitposts, but I think my point is understood.

So, I've started writing a short story, and it's actually a really entertaining endeavor. I'm not oozing ideas every moment I sit down to write it, but I do find myself wanting to come back to it, similar to as if I were reading an entertaining book. We'll see what comes of it, but I went to a writing workshop a couple weeks ago, and it was really cool to talk about my story with others who are also interested in writing.

If I'm on pace with Min Jin Lee's writing of Pachinko, my book should be out by the year 2054. See y'all then!

Back on my feet.
